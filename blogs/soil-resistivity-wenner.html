Soil Resistivity Measurement Using Wenner Method – Procedure & Importance


Soil resistivity is a fundamental parameter in the design of grounding systems. Accurate measurement of soil resistivity is essential for designing a safe and effective earthing system. The Wenner four-pin method is the most widely used technique for this purpose.

What is Soil Resistivity?

Soil resistivity is the resistance offered by soil to the flow of electric current. It directly affects the performance of grounding systems and determines how effectively fault current can be dissipated into the earth.

What is the Wenner Method?

The Wenner method is a field measurement technique that uses four equally spaced electrodes driven into the ground. A current is injected through the outer electrodes, and the voltage is measured across the inner electrodes to calculate soil resistivity.

Measurement Formula

Soil resistivity is calculated using the following formula:

ρ = 2πaR

Where:
ρ = Soil resistivity (ohm-meter)
a = Electrode spacing (meter)
R = Measured resistance (ohm)

Test Procedure

The standard procedure for Wenner method includes:

• Drive four electrodes into the ground at equal spacing
• Inject current through the outer electrodes
• Measure voltage across the inner electrodes
• Calculate resistance and determine soil resistivity
• Repeat for different spacings to analyze soil layers

Importance in Earthing Design

Soil resistivity data is essential for:

✔ Designing earthing grid size
✔ Determining conductor length
✔ Evaluating grounding resistance
✔ Ensuring compliance with safety standards

Practical Engineering Insight

Soil resistivity is not uniform and varies with depth, moisture, and temperature. Multiple measurements at different spacings are required to model soil layers accurately. This directly impacts grounding system performance.
